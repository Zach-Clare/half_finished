Remember walking home in the murky-blue air, the wet pavement reflecting bright headlights and scuffling under your feet. Your legs are aching and your breath condenses in the twilight, but home is just around the corner. And once it greets you, the stress of the day is done away with. Your ice-cold hands fumble with your keys and shortly after, you are welcomed with foggy windows and the smell of cooking food. 

To many, I'm sure longing after this feels absurd. To me, there's an indescribable attraction not only to the cosy feeling of finally getting home, but also to the actual cold, the actual pink freezing mornings, the actual dark late afternoons, the phosphorus yellow street-lights. Some of my favourite memories were not only in the snow, but in the rainy late-autumn days, the glittery fields brought by frozen morning dew, the list goes on.

Recently, I watched Louis Theroux's Dark States documentary. While it's a great documentary that you should absolutely go and watch, this particular scene from the opening moments caught me. This is a shot of Huntington, West Virginia, assumingly in early evening. It brought me back to walking the streets of a town in the English midlands when I was a boy. The streets look different, the architecture is different, the skyline, the landscape is completely different, yet this shot felt like home. I felt as if I had been here. 

It took me back to when I was living with a few of my friends in Derby. It was mid-November, around 5:30pm. I had finished work and decided to practise playing the piano in the dining room. Connected to that room was the kitchen, where a good friend of mine (who is also an excellent chef) was making a meal for all of us. At one point, I stopped playing and turned to the ceiling-height windows to my left, where all the daylight had stopped pouring in and had been replaced by this exact shade of blue. Passer-by's were hard to see because of the condensation on the window, and the occasional passing headlights were the only thing one could confidently make out. I reached for a jumper to keep me warm, and continued playing amongst the hungering aroma of an Italian dish.

Winter represents a slowing down, at least to me. It represents a rejection of "the hustle" and a wide-armed embrace of rest. In a world where we are pushed to be ever productive, where we feel pressure to monetize our hobbies and make use of daylight, this perceived acceptance of rest over never-ending work is as sweet as cinnamon. 

What's more difficult to grasp is the realisation that this acceptance comes from ourselves. We're more forgiving at certain times of the year than of others, and for what? A lesson I've recently been getting better at is to take that feeling with me throughout spring and summer. Slow down. Put your pyjamas on at 5pm. Make that drink you like and rest up. The days are short, but life is long. Remember to feel it.
